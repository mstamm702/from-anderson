No. 11 Marketing Strategy MGMT 411  ·  Group Project

The Customer's Relationship with Time Changes Everything

One brand. Two locations. Two completely different marketing strategies. Not because the product changed. Because the customer's relationship with time did.

The Framework
PULL Korean Corn Dog and the Two-Segment Problem

PULL is a Korean corn dog concept designed for two Los Angeles locations: Venice Beach Boardwalk and Third Street Promenade. Same product. Same brand. Same core menu. Completely different markets.

The group project required developing a full marketing plan for both locations, which meant working through the STP framework twice and arriving at two distinct strategies from the same starting point. The discipline of doing this work in parallel made something visible that is easy to miss when you only have one market: the customer's relationship with time is the variable that drives almost every downstream strategic decision.

Venice Beach draws 28,000 to 30,000 visitors per day, the majority of them locals aged 25 to 34. The primary target at Venice is the Local Regular, someone who lives or works nearby, visits frequently, and is motivated by community, discovery, and drop culture. A Local Regular might visit PULL 50 or more times in a year. Their CLV is substantial. The marketing strategy at Venice is built around retention, loyalty, and the weekly limited-edition drop ritual. Pricing is set below EVC to drive repeat visit frequency. The drop is the promotion.

Third Street Promenade draws approximately 438,000 annual visitors, heavily tourist, with foot traffic of around 1,200 per day. The primary target at the Promenade is the Experience Tourist, someone visiting LA from out of state, seeking something novel and distinctly local, not price sensitive, buying once. Their entire customer journey from awareness to purchase must happen in a single walk-by. Pricing is set closer to EVC because the one-time transaction has to capture the full value. The storefront is the ad.

The core insight is clean: the Local Regular has infinite future interactions with PULL, so optimize for long-term loyalty. The Experience Tourist has one interaction, maybe ever, so optimize for maximizing the value of that single moment. Every difference in the two marketing plans flows from that one root cause.

The Application
Time Horizon Is a Strategic Variable, Not a Background Assumption

Most marketing organizations build their strategies around the product and the channel. The PULL case suggests a more useful starting point: what is the customer's time horizon with us, and what does that mean for how we acquire, serve, and retain them?

The distinction between a customer you are trying to earn over many interactions and a customer you have one chance to win plays out across almost every marketing context. A loyalty program member who has been enrolled for three years and visits monthly has a completely different CLV and a completely different strategic value than someone who downloaded the app for a one-time promotion and has not engaged since. The marketing investment, the offer design, and the retention strategy that makes sense for the first person will not make sense for the second.

Similarly, a co-branded credit card acquisition campaign targeting high-intent applicants who are likely to become long-term cardholders is a fundamentally different investment from a campaign designed to drive a single high-value transaction. Both are legitimate goals. But applying the same strategy to both is the equivalent of running a drop culture retention play at the Promenade or a tourist-facing conversion play in Venice. The tactics do not match the time horizon.

The discipline the PULL case builds is the habit of asking, before anything else: how long does this customer have to be worth it?

The Question Worth Asking

Does our strategy change based on what the customer's relationship with time actually is, or are we applying the same playbook regardless of whether we are trying to earn someone's loyalty or win a single moment?
