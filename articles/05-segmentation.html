Not All Customers Are Created Equal

Volume is not value. The right segmentation question is not who can we reach, but who should we serve and what does it cost us when we get that wrong.

The Framework
Natureview Farm and the Pressure to Grow

Natureview Farm was a small Vermont organic yogurt brand with a real problem. Their VC funding was ending and they had 18 months to grow from roughly $13 million in revenue to $20 million or more. The pressure to act fast pointed toward the obvious answer: expand into conventional grocery chains and capture the volume that the natural channel could not deliver on its own.

But volume and value are not the same thing, and the Natureview case is one of the cleaner illustrations of why that distinction matters so much.

The market had three distinct segments. Core Organic Loyalists were already in the natural channel, loyal, premium-paying, and deeply aligned with the Natureview brand. Health-Curious Mainstream consumers were in conventional grocery, price-sensitive, and not yet committed to organic. Health-Conscious Parents were cross-channel shoppers for whom organic ingredient safety in children's food was non-negotiable. They were premium buyers and, once earned, loyal.

The instinct under revenue pressure is to go after the biggest pool. In this case that meant conventional grocery and Health-Curious Mainstream shoppers. More stores, more volume, faster to the number. The problem is that segment drives margin through scale, and Natureview did not have the broker infrastructure, trade promotion budget, or manufacturing capacity to win on those terms against Dannon and Horizon.

The better answer was Health-Conscious Parents. Smaller addressable pool but highest willingness to pay, strongest fit with Natureview's most differentiated product, and durable loyalty once trust was established. Two supermarket chains, four SKUs anchored by the Children's Multi-Pack, projected $12.3 million in gross revenue uplift and a path to $25 million total. Financially sound and strategically defensible.

The framework is straightforward: segment the market by wants, behaviors, and willingness to pay. Target the segment where your differentiation is strongest and switching costs are highest. Do not let revenue pressure pull you toward the segment that is merely largest.

The Application
What This Means for How We Think About Customers

The Natureview tension shows up in any growth-oriented marketing organization. Leadership sets a revenue target. The team looks for the fastest path to that number. The fastest path usually means reaching the broadest possible audience. And that is where the segmentation discipline breaks down.

Broad reach is not a bad goal. But it becomes a problem when it replaces the question of who the right customer actually is. A loyalty program, a digital product, a promotional offer, a co-branded credit card, each of these has a segment it was built for. When the target shifts toward whoever is easiest to acquire rather than whoever is most valuable to serve, you can end up spending significantly to attract customers who were never going to behave the way the model assumed.

The Natureview lesson is that going deeper on the right segment almost always outperforms going wider on the wrong one. Two chains done well beat six chains done thinly. The Children's Multi-Pack in the right hands is worth more than the 8-oz cup on every shelf in the country.

The discipline is in resisting the pull toward volume and asking instead: who is this actually for, what do they value, and are we building toward that?

The Question Worth Asking

Are we optimizing for the customer who is easiest to reach, or the one who is worth the most to serve?
