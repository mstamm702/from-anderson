Designing Ethical Systems

The Wells Fargo scandal was not a story about bad people. It was a story about a system that made bad behavior rational. The difference matters more than most organizations acknowledge.

The Framework
The Wells Fargo Case: When the System Is the Problem

Between 2002 and 2016, Wells Fargo employees opened millions of unauthorized customer accounts. The initial narrative was that the bank had hired dishonest people or allowed a culture of dishonesty to take root. The more uncomfortable and more accurate analysis is different.

Wells Fargo had an aggressive cross-selling target, tracked daily, visible to everyone in the organization, tied directly to compensation, and essentially impossible to hit through legitimate means at the volume required. The system created conditions in which crossing ethical lines was the rational path to job security and financial reward. Most of the people who opened unauthorized accounts were not bad people. They were people responding to incentives.

The behavior you get is a function of the system you build, not just the values you espouse. An organization can have the right stated values, the right training programs, the right compliance policies, and still produce systematically unethical behavior if the incentive structures point in a different direction.

The questions to ask are not just what did people do wrong but what did the system make rational. Who designed the incentives, what behaviors did those incentives reward, and were the people designing them thinking clearly about the second-order effects of what they were building.

The corrective is equally structural. Aligning incentives with the behaviors you actually want requires auditing not just what you measure but what you reward, what you ignore, and what you implicitly permit.

The Application
Incentive Design as a Leadership Responsibility

This framework applies to any organization that sets targets, measures performance, and ties rewards to outcomes. Which is every organization.

The relevant questions for a marketing organization are specific. What are the metrics that matter most in performance reviews and compensation decisions? Are those metrics measuring what we actually value, or are they measuring what is easy to count? And critically: if someone on the team were optimizing purely for those metrics, what would they do and would we endorse those behaviors?

Vanity metrics, attribution models that reward the last touch regardless of influence, acquisition targets that do not account for customer quality, and short-term revenue goals that conflict with long-term loyalty economics are all examples of incentive structures that can produce rational but ultimately harmful behavior.

Designing ethical systems is not primarily an HR or compliance function. It is a leadership function. The people who set targets and design measurement frameworks are, whether they think of it this way or not, designing the incentive architecture that shapes what the organization actually does.

The Question Worth Asking

If we looked honestly at our incentive structures, what behaviors are we rewarding that we would not endorse if we saw them plainly?
