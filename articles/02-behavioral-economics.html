Your Customers Are Not Rational and That Is the Point

Behavioral economics does not describe how people should make decisions. It describes how they actually do. For marketers, that is not a warning. It is the job.

The Framework
The Frameworks That Explain Real Consumer Behavior

Classical economics assumed a rational actor: a person who evaluates all available options, weighs costs and benefits accurately, and makes the choice that maximizes their utility. Behavioral economics showed that this model is a useful fiction. People use mental shortcuts. They are loss-averse. They anchor to the first number they see. They make different choices depending on how options are framed, even when the underlying math is identical.

Anchoring is the tendency to rely heavily on the first piece of information encountered. If the first price a customer sees is high, everything after that feels like a deal. Loss aversion means that the pain of losing something is roughly twice as powerful as the pleasure of gaining something equivalent. This is why free trials work better than discounts.

The endowment effect means people value things more once they own them. Getting a customer to experience ownership, even briefly, increases willingness to pay. Choice architecture is the idea that how options are presented shapes which option is chosen, independent of the options themselves. Default options are selected at dramatically higher rates than non-defaults.

None of this means customers are being manipulated. It means that every design decision about how an offer, an experience, or a communication is structured is also a decision about behavior. The question is whether that design is intentional or accidental.

The Application
Applying Behavioral Insight to Offers, UX, and Communications

The behavioral economics toolkit is directly applicable to how we design loyalty program offers, digital product experiences, and marketing communications.

In loyalty programs, the near-miss effect, the feeling of being close to a reward threshold, is a documented driver of engagement and incremental spend. Showing members how close they are to the next tier or the next reward is not just informational. It is motivational in a way that a simple points balance is not.

In digital UX, defaults matter enormously. What is pre-selected, what is shown first, and what requires an extra click all shape behavior at scale. In communications, framing effects mean that the same offer communicated differently produces meaningfully different response rates. An offer framed as what you will gain and an offer framed as what you will lose if you do not act are not equivalent, even if the economic value is identical.

The Question Worth Asking

Where in our customer experience are we designing as if people are rational when the evidence suggests they are not?
