Know Your Core

Harley Davidson built a new brand and a new product to capture a younger audience. They lost the new segment and nearly lost the one they already had. The lesson was expensive. It did not have to be.

The Framework
What Harley Davidson Taught Us About Audience Strategy

In the 1980s and 1990s, Harley Davidson faced a real strategic question. Their core customer was aging. Leadership looked ahead, saw a demographic cliff, and concluded they needed to attract a younger rider. Reasonable instinct. Reasonable urgency. The execution is where it went wrong.

Rather than finding a way to bring a younger audience into the Harley world, they created something separate. A new brand. A new product designed to appeal to a segment that had not yet expressed interest in what Harley stood for. They stripped away the chrome. They softened the identity. They built something that was, in effect, a diluted version of the thing that had made Harley iconic.

The younger segment did not come. And the existing audience, the core riders who had built Harley into a cultural institution, felt alienated. A brand that had meant something specific now seemed uncertain about what it was. That uncertainty is hard to recover from.

What the case illustrates is a pattern that shows up across industries: the instinct to expand audience can, if handled carelessly, erode the very identity that made the brand worth expanding. The solution Harley eventually found was not to abandon the pursuit of younger riders. It was to lean harder into what made Harley Harley, and create a variant of the brand that was authentically Harley but accessible to a new entrant. Stripped down. Raw. Still unmistakably Harley. That product attracted the younger segment. Because it was real.

The same pattern is visible in Harley's current challenge with electric bikes. A different era, a different product category, but the same underlying question: how do you pursue a new audience without losing the one that defines you?

The Application
When Growth Pressure Meets Brand Identity

This case resonates in any organization where leadership regularly challenges the team to grow the audience, reach a new segment, or attract a customer who does not currently look like the current customer. That pressure is legitimate. Audience concentration is a real risk. But the Harley case is a useful check on how that pressure gets translated into strategy.

The question worth asking before any audience expansion initiative is not just who do we want to attract? It is what is the thing we are asking them to join? If the answer to that second question is unclear, or if the answer changes depending on who you are trying to attract, that is a signal worth pausing on.

The brands and products that successfully expand their audience almost always do it by going deeper into their identity, not by softening it. They find the version of themselves that speaks to a new person without requiring the existing person to wonder what happened to the thing they loved.

The Question Worth Asking

When we design for the audience we want, are we building something that the audience we have would still recognize as ours?
