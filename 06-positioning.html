Business Model Disruption

Domino's did not become a better pizza company. It became a technology company that sells pizza. The distinction is not semantic. It is the whole strategy.

The Framework
The Domino's Case: Reinventing the Operating Model

By the late 2000s, Domino's was in trouble. Their core product was widely regarded as inferior. Customer satisfaction scores were low, sales were declining, and the brand was losing ground to competitors who were iterating faster. The obvious move was to fix the pizza. And they did. But that was not the strategic decision that changed the trajectory of the company.

What Domino's actually did was rebuild the entire operating model around digital. They invested heavily in technology infrastructure, online ordering, delivery logistics, and data. They built systems that allowed customers to track orders in real time, order from any device, and interact with the brand entirely without human contact if they preferred. They treated software as a core competency, not a marketing feature.

The result was that Domino's outperformed every major pizza competitor for years not because their pizza became the best in the world but because their system became the most frictionless. By 2017 they were being compared to technology companies.

The framework here is about where in the value chain a company chooses to compete. Domino's could have competed on product quality alone. Instead they competed on the operating model that surrounds the product. Delivery speed, order accuracy, digital convenience, and data-driven personalization became the differentiation.

Product innovation makes the thing better. Business model innovation changes the system that delivers the thing. Both matter. But business model innovation is harder to replicate and tends to produce more durable competitive advantage.

The Application
Digital Transformation Is a Model Question, Not a Channel Question

The Domino's case is a useful lens for any organization navigating digital transformation. The temptation is to treat digital as a channel, a place where marketing happens, a way to reach customers. The more consequential question is whether digital is changing the operating model itself.

For gaming and entertainment, the shift from retail-only to omnichannel is well underway. The question is whether digital is being built as a channel layer on top of an existing model or as a structural rethinking of how customers are acquired, served, retained, and monetized. Those are very different investments with very different returns.

Domino's teaches that the companies who win the digital transformation are often not the ones with the best product. They are the ones who build the best system around the product and make that system itself the reason customers stay.

The Question Worth Asking

If we stripped away everything except the thing our best customers actually come for, what would be left and is that what we are building toward?
