No. 13 Marketing Strategy MGMT 411  ·  Final Exam

What Business Are You Actually In?

Peloton spent years telling the world it was a hardware company. The financial statements told a different story the entire time. The real business, the one with 67.5% gross margins and durable retention, was software.

The Framework
The Peloton Case: When the Product Is Not the Business

Peloton's revenue peak was $4.0 billion in FY2021, driven by a pandemic-era surge in connected fitness hardware. By FY2025, connected hardware revenue had collapsed to $1.0 billion. Subscription revenue, meanwhile, had grown to $1.7 billion at a 67.5% gross margin, with 74% share of connected fitness subscriptions and 66% of subscribers who joined in 2021 still active years later. Members averaged 13 or more monthly workouts per paid subscription, a level of behavioral engagement that almost no consumer product achieves.

The MGMT 411 final exam posed a specific strategic question: should Peloton introduce PeloTone, a full-body strength station, or PeloVerse, an immersive VR fitness app? The choice required taking a position on whether Peloton is better positioned as a hardware company or a software and media technology company.

The analysis pointed clearly to software. Peloton's competitive moat is not its equipment. It is 42,000 classes across 16 modalities, 57 global instructors, 6.6 million members, and NPS scores of 67 to 78 that reflect deep emotional attachment to the content and community, not the machine. Hardware serves one strategic purpose: driving subscription attachment at 67.5% gross margin. PeloTone is not a hardware bet. It is a software acquisition vehicle.

The recommendation was PeloTone, priced at $2,995 hardware plus $44 per month subscription, positioned $5 below competitor Tonal and generating $565 in customer surplus. EVC against Tonal as the reference product came out to $4,088. CLV for the target segment came out to $4,155 at a $300 assumed customer acquisition cost, a CLV/CAC ratio of 13.85 times, well above the 3x industry benchmark. The pricing logic: hardware margin is intentionally thin. All value capture happens through the subscription. The hardware is the front door.

The right target segment was high-income home gym enthusiasts not yet in the Peloton ecosystem, households earning $100K or more aged 35 to 54 who already invest in home fitness but have not yet committed to Peloton. That gap between Peloton's roughly 900,000 household share of market and the 24 million household serviceable addressable market represents the highest-value net new revenue opportunity in the portfolio.

The Application
The Hardware-Software Question Is Not Unique to Peloton

The question Peloton has been forced to answer explicitly, what business are we actually in, is one that every product and loyalty organization should be asking on a regular basis. The answer is rarely obvious when the physical product is the most visible thing the company makes.

For a gaming and entertainment company, the physical asset is the casino property. It is where the customer shows up, where the experience happens, and where the majority of capital is deployed. But the relationship that drives long-term revenue is not the property. It is the loyalty program, the digital product, the personalized offers, and the behavioral data that allow the organization to serve the right customer the right experience at the right moment. The property is the front door. The loyalty relationship is the business.

This is the same structural logic as Peloton. The bike gets the customer in. The subscription keeps them. And the economic value of the subscription, recurring revenue at high gross margin with durable retention, is what the business is actually worth. Pricing, marketing investment, and product development decisions that treat the hardware or the property as the primary value driver will consistently underinvest in the part of the business that actually compounds over time.

The Peloton case makes this concrete with numbers. A customer acquired for PeloTone at $300 CAC is worth $4,155 in CLV through subscription revenue alone. The hardware is priced to generate customer surplus and drive adoption. The margin is in the relationship that follows. Understanding which part of the business you are actually in changes where you put your money, your talent, and your attention.

The Question Worth Asking

If we stripped away our physical product entirely, what would remain, and is that the business we are actually investing in building?
