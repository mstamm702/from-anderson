Price to What It Is Worth, Not What It Cost

The gap between what something costs to produce and what it is worth to the customer is where margin lives. Most pricing decisions leave that gap on the table.

The Framework
The Curled Metal Case: A Clinic in Value-Based Pricing

Curled Metal Inc. developed a proprietary cushion pad for pile driving that dramatically outperformed conventional alternatives. The CMI pad lasted significantly longer, required far fewer replacements, reduced downtime, improved energy transfer, and lowered total project cost for contractors. It was a genuinely superior product.

The pricing question the case poses is deceptively simple: what should CMI charge? The cost-plus answer put the price around $1,250 to $1,300 per set of six. But the Economic Value to the Customer calculation told a very different story.

One CMI pad set could replace approximately 20 conventional sets in a 15,000-foot job. At $150 per conventional set, the reference price alone was $3,000. Add downtime savings from reduced pad changes: roughly $4,700 at $714 per hour of equipment time. Add productivity savings from a 33% improvement in driving speed: roughly $17,850. Total EVC per set: $25,000 to $27,000.

CMI's recommendation was to price at $15,000 per six-pad set. Not at cost plus. Not at full EVC. At a price that captured meaningful value for CMI while leaving the majority of the economic benefit with the contractor, which was the right strategy for driving adoption in a conservative, price-anchored industry.

The framework: cost sets the floor, competition sets a reference point, and customer value sets the ceiling. Pricing between the floor and the ceiling is a strategic choice, not a formula.

The Application
Value-Based Pricing in Marketing and Loyalty Contexts

The CMI case is about industrial equipment but the pricing framework is universal. Any time a product or offer delivers measurable value to the customer, cost-plus pricing is leaving money on the table and competitive benchmarking is answering the wrong question.

For a loyalty program, the value-based pricing lens applies to offer design as much as traditional pricing. What is a points offer actually worth to the member? What is the economic value of a tier benefit, a bonus redemption, a co-brand card welcome offer? If the answer is based on what it costs to fund the offer rather than what it is worth to the recipient, the offer is probably either undervalued or overpriced relative to its behavioral impact.

For a co-branded credit card, the same logic applies to how the program is priced to the issuing bank and how the card benefits are calibrated for the cardholder. The relevant question is not what is the cost of the rewards we are providing but what is the value of the loyalty behavior those rewards are designed to generate.

The Curled Metal lesson is that understanding the customer's economics is what makes value-based pricing possible. You cannot price to customer value if you do not know what that value is.

The Question Worth Asking

Are we pricing to our cost and competitive benchmarks, or to the economic value we actually deliver to the customer?
